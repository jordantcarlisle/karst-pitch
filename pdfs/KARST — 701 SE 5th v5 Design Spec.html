KARST at 701 SE 5th Street

v5 Site Analysis & Financial Model — Dual-Entity Structure

Builds on v4 (Destination Edition) with a two-entity capital structure separating real estate investment from operating risk. Real Estate Co. acquires and develops the property, holding all capital assets. KARST Operating Co. leases the finished space, handling only pre-opening and operational costs. This separation creates two distinct investor vehicles with different risk profiles and time horizons — making both easier to fund.

Additional v5 updates: wrap-around exterior patio (5th Street to F Street, trail-activated on two frontages), enhanced event revenue strategy anchored to the Momentary campus, and multi-family apartment membership partnerships.

3. Capital Structure — Two-Entity Model

v5 KEY CHANGE. The project is structured as two separate legal entities with distinct investor pools, risk profiles, and time horizons.

3.1 The Two Entities

Real Estate Co. (PropCo) KARST Operating Co. (OpCo)
Purpose Acquire, develop, and hold the property Lease and operate the wellness facility
Investor thesis Real estate appreciation on a 10-year horizon Operating business reaching break-even by Year 3
Capital required ~$7.3M (midpoint) ~$510K (midpoint)
Risk profile Lower — hard asset with appreciation floor Higher — operating/venture risk, execution-dependent
Time horizon 10 years 3-year break-even target
Downside protection Property appreciates 2–3x over 10 years; generalizable improvements retain value for any tenant Limited to operating reserve and pivot optionality

3.2 Why Separate

The core insight: real estate risk and operating risk attract different capital. Combining them forces every investor to accept both risk profiles — narrowing the pool and complicating the raise.

Separated:

  • Real Estate Co. investors are underwriting a downtown Bentonville property at a 10-year hold. They understand property appreciation, debt paydown, and re-tenanting. The KARST operating business is upside, not the thesis. These are traditional real estate investors comfortable with patient capital and modest cash-on-cash returns backed by hard-asset appreciation.

  • KARST OpCo investors are underwriting a wellness operating business with ~$510K in startup capital. They don’t need to evaluate a $7.3M real estate deal. Their risk is execution: can KARST reach break-even by Year 3? The capital requirement is an order of magnitude smaller, and the return on invested capital is dramatically higher.

This separation makes both raises easier. The property investors don’t need to believe in the wellness concept — they need to believe in Bentonville real estate. The KARST investors don’t need to fund $7M+ — they need to fund a pre-opening runway.

3.3 What Each Entity Owns

Real Estate Co. develops and holds: - Property acquisition ($3.5M) - All construction — generalizable improvements (parking, rooftop structure, mezzanine loft) AND KARST-specific buildout (thermal, fitness/reception area) - Soft costs (architect, engineering, permits, project management) - FF&E (thermal systems, cold plunge, fitness equipment, furniture, technology, signage) - Site work (parking, patio, garden, landscaping, bike parking, stormwater)

The KARST-specific buildout and FF&E are held by the Real Estate Co. because they represent capital improvements to the property. In a downside scenario, the thermal infrastructure has re-use value for any wellness/spa operator; the fitness buildout serves any gym tenant. Keeping all capital assets in PropCo maximizes the property’s standalone value and simplifies the OpCo’s balance sheet.

Note: If the investor structure is better served by keeping KARST-specific buildout costs (thermal, FF&E, programming-specific site work) with the OpCo, these line items can shift. The generalizable improvements (parking, rooftop, mezzanine) should always remain with the Real Estate Co.

KARST OpCo funds: - Pre-opening costs (operating reserve, marketing launch, insurance, staff hiring + training)

3.4 Lease Terms Between Entities

Period Rent Structure Annual Rent Rationale
Years 1–3 (ramp to break-even) At-cost ~$750,000 Covers Real Estate Co.’s carrying costs (debt service, property tax, insurance, basic maintenance). KARST is not yet profitable — at-cost rent protects the operating business during ramp-up.
Year 3+ (post break-even) Premium ~$950,000 At-cost + ~$200K annual distribution margin. Triggered when KARST reaches break-even profitability. KARST and Real Estate Co. agree to the premium rate, enabling shareholder distributions to PropCo investors.

The transition from at-cost to premium rent is contractually defined but practically flexible — both entities share founders, and the goal is to reward PropCo investors once the operating business can sustain the premium.

3.6 Downside Protection — Real Estate Co.

If KARST does not reach break-even by Year 3:

  1. At-cost rent continues. The Real Estate Co. is not losing money — its carrying costs are covered by at-cost rent. The property is held, not distressed.

  2. Re-tenanting optionality. The generalizable improvements (parking, rooftop with operable walls, mezzanine loft with sound isolation, upgraded mechanical/electrical) make the property attractive to a range of commercial tenants: coworking, event venue, restaurant/bar, fitness, retail. The building is more valuable than when acquired.

  3. Property appreciation. Downtown Bentonville real estate within 0.5 miles of Walmart HQ has a strong appreciation trajectory. Over a 10-year hold, the property is likely worth 2–3x the acquisition price — independent of any operating tenant’s success.

  4. Debt paydown. Even at breakeven (at-cost rent covering carrying costs), the Real Estate Co. is paying down principal on the property loan, building equity each year.

The worst case for PropCo investors: KARST operates at-cost for several years, the property appreciates, and PropCo sells or re-tenants at a gain. The operating company’s failure does not impair the real estate thesis.

5.5 Event & Partnership Strategy (NEW in v5)

Momentary Festival & Concert Overlays

The rooftop (Sky deck) provides a direct sightline overlooking the Momentary campus. This is a unique, non-replicable asset. During Momentary events — Format festival, Wavelength, major concerts, gallery openings — the KARST rooftop becomes a premium viewing and hospitality position.

Strategy: - Pre-sell private event packages around every major Momentary festival and concert. Lock in corporate hosts, wellness brands, and VIP groups weeks in advance. - Build a parallel calendar. As the Momentary’s lineup of festivals and engagements continues to improve, KARST’s event revenue grows in lockstep — without bearing any programming cost. - Premium pricing. Momentary overlay events command 2–3x standard event rental rates. The view is the product.

Wellness Brand Activations

The Walmart vendor community in Bentonville creates a unique B2B channel. Health and wellness brands — supplements, equipment manufacturers, apparel, wearable health tech — routinely travel to Bentonville for Walmart buyer meetings.

Strategy: - Product launch events. Brands hosting product launches, partnership announcements, or buyer showcases can rent the rooftop and canopy space to engage the Walmart community in an immersive wellness environment. - Experiential marketing. A new recovery wearable or supplement line launching in Walmart is more compelling when the buyer experiences it post-sauna, on a rooftop overlooking the Momentary, with Ozark views. - Targeted outreach. KARST’s sales team (or a partnerships role) builds relationships with wellness brand marketing teams and Walmart category buyers. The pitch: “Your next Bentonville event belongs at KARST.”

9. Strategic Assessment

What This Property Does Well

  • Location is prime. Adjacent to Greenway, 0.5 miles from Walmart HQ, 1 block from The Momentary. Crystal Bridges sends 785K+ visitors/year to a neighborhood that stops at the door of this building.

  • Block walls + concrete ceiling = perfect thermal envelope. Heavy construction creates naturally cave-like conditions. Minimal additional insulation needed.

  • Ceiling height enables the loft. 800 sf of enclosed studio space from vertical space that already exists — cheapest square footage in the build.

  • Corner lot = multiple frontages. Wrap-around patio from 5th Street to F Street creates a public presence on two streets. The F Street segment runs alongside the Razorback Greenway — trail users see KARST from both the street and the trail. Primary destination marketing is free.

  • Guaranteed sunset views. Non-buildable lot to the west ensures unobstructed views indefinitely.

  • Trail adjacency is the distribution channel. The Razorback Greenway delivers athletes — the primary day pass demographic — directly to the front door. Bike parking is the first thing they see.

  • Momentary adjacency is the event multiplier. The rooftop overlooks the Momentary campus. Every Momentary festival, concert, and gallery opening drives foot traffic past KARST’s front door AND creates premium event rental demand on the rooftop. KARST’s event calendar grows as the Momentary’s programming improves — zero programming cost to KARST.

  • Walmart vendor corridor. Health and wellness brands visiting Bentonville for Walmart buyer meetings have no premium venue for product launches and experiential marketing. KARST fills that gap — the only space in Bentonville where a wellness brand can host an immersive product activation within walking distance of Walmart HQ.

  • Price is known. $4M asking / $3.5M target is a real number to underwrite against.

What This Property Constrains

  • Scale. 5,400 sf enclosed is a focused facility, not a resort. Members wanting a full traditional gym go elsewhere — by design. KARST is not a gym. It is a place.

  • Parking. ~30–35 spaces on 0.52 acres. Trail access, hotel guest foot traffic, and multi-family apartment walkability partially compensate, but parking strategy needs attention at high day pass volume.

  • No underground excavation. Ozark limestone substrate makes deep excavation impractical. The cave aesthetic is achieved through design, not geology — dim lighting, stone cladding, block wall character. This is a feature.

  • Growth ceiling. Once this lot is built out, expansion requires adjacent parcels or relocation. First location proves the model; multi-location development is the growth path.

Critical Path

  1. Structure the dual-entity model. Engage securities attorney to draft the Real Estate Co. and KARST OpCo operating agreements. Define the at-cost → premium rent transition terms. Budget $15–25K for PPM (Private Placement Memorandum) — may need one per entity.

  2. Preliminary structural assessment. $5,000–$10,000 before committing. Non-negotiable first step.

  3. Raise PropCo capital. The real estate pitch is straightforward: downtown Bentonville property, 10-year hold, 2–3x appreciation thesis, tenant in place from day one. Target real estate investors comfortable with patient capital.

  4. Raise OpCo capital. The KARST pitch is compelling: ~$510K startup capital, $1.1M+ annual net at maturity, < 6 months payback. Target strategic investors — wellness industry, Bentonville insiders, brand-aligned capital.

  5. Launch KARST as a brand immediately. National brand recognition is the Year 1 success metric. The brand must exist before the building. Start the podcast, social channels, and Cave of Connection story now.

  6. Lock in 2 hotel partnerships + 2 apartment partnerships. Hotel concierge relationships before opening create a day pass floor. Apartment partnerships before opening create a membership floor. Both de-risk the destination model projections.

  7. Pre-sell Momentary overlay events. Lock in event rental commitments for the first two Momentary festivals after opening. Demonstrates the rooftop event thesis to investors.

  8. Run the founding member presale. The next 5 pop-up events are KARST events — branded, filmed, connected to a founding membership waitlist. 100 founding members pre-sold before groundbreaking changes the entire investor conversation.

  9. Research trip. Visit the Russian & Turkish Baths (NYC), Aire Ancient Baths (NYC or Chicago), and one international destination (Löyly Helsinki or Therme Bucharest). Document the ritual, the architecture, the business model. The Aufguss ceremony needs to be experienced before it is designed.
